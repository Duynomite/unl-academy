Run the Call

Six full annotated master-call scripts. From opener to application transition. Side-by-side with the commentary that reveals what a great agent does and why.

This is where mastery actually happens. Each script is a complete 4-6 minute call with 25-40 exchanges of dialogue, persona context, real-time annotations on every move, and a post-call breakdown. ▸ How to read these scripts

Each script is structured the same way: a persona setup at the top (lead source, customer details, pre-call state), the full call transcript with stage markers and inline annotations, and a post-call analysis identifying the master moves and the common mistakes the agent avoided.

Read each script the way you'd watch a chess master play a game — not for the words exactly, but for the patterns. Annotations appear after the moves that matter; read them carefully on first pass, skim them on second pass when you come back for muscle memory.

A
Journey 1 · Path 1a · Cross-sell after MA close

Robert — the seamless transition

Robert just enrolled in a new MA plan with you. Trust is at its peak — he's about to hang up. This is the moment most agents wave him goodbye and miss the easiest cross-sell of the day. Watch how the master agent leverages the SOA she captured at call start to bridge into HI + HHC without it ever feeling like a separate pitch.

Pre-call setup
Lead source
SMID-approved inbound call (Medicare Advantage creative)
Persona
Robert, 68, retired carpenter, FL
Household
Lives with wife Linda (65); son in Atlanta, daughter near Asheville
Income
$1,900/mo Social Security + small union pension
Prescriptions
4 monthly — atorvastatin, lisinopril, metformin (generic) + Trulicity (brand)
Fit filters
No Medicaid · No Medigap · Both products in play
Verbal SOA
Captured at call start: MA, MAPD, PDP, HI, HHC all "yes"
Pre-call state
25 min into call. UHC PPO MA plan just enrolled. Cross-sell window open.
Transition 25:30
Agent
"Great — you're locked in. UHC will have your card to you in about 7 to 10 days. Last thing before I let you go, Robert — at the start of our call you agreed I could also talk to you about Hospital Indemnity and Home Healthcare. Your new plan still leaves you with a $300/day copay if you get admitted, and Medicare itself doesn't cover daily-living help at home. Couple quick questions to see if either one's a fit?"
The transition uses three load-bearing words: "Last thing." The customer feels like the call is ending — but trust is at its highest point of the entire conversation. The agent leverages the verbal SOA she captured at call start, so no new compliance step is needed. The bridge names the gaps without pitching: copay exposure on the new plan + Medicare's home-care gap. "Couple quick questions" sets a time expectation. Notice she doesn't say "let me tell you about" — she says "see if either one's a fit." Permission-asking, not pushing.
Robert
"Yeah, that's fine."
Stage 2A · Discovery 26:15
Agent
"Tell me about your prescriptions — how many are you filling regularly?"
Robert
"Four. Atorvastatin, lisinopril, metformin… and a Trulicity injection."
Agent
"Got it. The metformin and Trulicity — that's for diabetes?"
Woven HI knockout Q1b. The agent doesn't say "I need to check your knockouts." She just asks the natural follow-up to a Rx list that includes a diabetes med. Robert has no idea he's being screened — feels like genuine interest.
Robert
"Yeah, type 2. Got diagnosed about eight years ago. The Trulicity helps with the weight too."
Agent
"Type 2, managed with oral and the injection. Are you on insulin at all, or just the Trulicity?"
The actual HI Q1b check. Trulicity is a GLP-1, not insulin — so it's NOT a knockout. The agent asks directly without making it sound like an interrogation by framing it as confirming what's already been said.
Robert
"No, no insulin. Doctor said the Trulicity's working well enough we don't need to go there."
Agent
"Good — keep that going. And those four prescriptions, you fill them monthly?"
Robert
"Every month at the Publix pharmacy down the street."
Agent
"Perfect. Quick one — is your wife home with you most of the time? Any kids nearby?"
Robert
"Linda's home, yeah. Kids are spread out — son's in Atlanta with his family, daughter's near Asheville. We see 'em a couple times a year if we're lucky."
Family discovery in one question. The "kids spread out" detail just became load-bearing — there's no nearby family who could realistically step in if Robert or Linda needed care. The agent is building a mental model of his situation while he thinks he's just chatting.
Stage 2C · The Preference Question 28:00
Agent
"If you ever needed care, Robert — like after a fall or a stroke or something — would you rather stay home, or go into a facility?"
Robert
"Home. Hundred percent home."
Agent
"Say more about that — what makes home the answer for you?"
Looping. The agent doesn't acknowledge with "got it" — she asks him to articulate why. The second articulation is always more emotional than the first. This is where the prospect builds the case for the product without realizing it.
Robert
"Hell, I built this house with my own two hands in '92. Linda and I raised our kids here. We have our routine. The thought of being in some sterile facility with strangers wiping me down? No thanks."
That answer is gold. Robert has now articulated the HHC value proposition more powerfully than any script could write for him. The agent lets the answer hang. She does not add to it. She lets him hear himself.
Agent
"Yeah, I get that. Quick one — do you currently get any help around the house? Bathing, dressing, anything like that?"
Woven HHC Q2 + HI Q2 knockouts. Doesn't sound like underwriting — sounds like follow-up to the home conversation.
Robert
"No, no, I'm still kicking. Bad knees but I get around fine."
Stage 2B · Problem Awareness 29:00
Agent
"Good. Let me ask you this — your new plan has a $300/day copay for the first 5 days if you ever get admitted. A 5-day stay, that's $1,500 in copays. If something happened tomorrow, what would $1,500 in surprise bills do to your situation?"
Robert
"Oh that'd hurt. We're living on Social Security and a small pension. Got a little savings but not much. That'd be a real punch."
Agent
"Yeah. And on the home care side — Medicare home health is great for the skilled stuff, nursing visits, therapies. Family-burden framing. The agent connects the product to the answer Robert gave 90 seconds ago about "kids spread out." The call has been quietly building this case the whole time. Robert now sees the gap himself.
Knockout gate 30:00
Agent
"Before I walk you through specifically how this would work for you, let me confirm a couple of things to make sure it's a fit. In the last 24 months, anything serious health-wise? Cancer, heart attack, stroke, anything like that?"
The explicit hard-knockout gate. Notice the framing: "before I walk you through specifically how this would work FOR YOU." The promise of personalized info comes AFTER the knockouts. Customer is now invested.
Robert
"No. Knees are bad and the diabetes, but otherwise I've been lucky."
Agent
"Breathing issues at all — COPD, oxygen?"
Robert
"No, none of that."
Agent

Robert
"Nope. Sharp as I was at 40. Maybe sharper."

Agent

Robert
"Yeah, go ahead."

Agent
"Here's how it works for you. The Hospital Indemnity side — about $35 a month. If you get admitted, it pays YOU cash directly. $300 a day for the first 5 days — so a 5-day stay puts $1,500 right back in your pocket. Copays, groceries, anything. Does that solve the hospital piece?"
Robert
"Yeah, that's clean."
Agent
"Good. Now the Home Healthcare side — this is the bigger piece. Plan B fits you. $100,000 in home care coverage if you ever need it, $300 a day for up to a year. Plus — and this is the part most folks miss — if your daughter or anyone ends up taking care of you, the plan pays them $3,500 directly. So she's not on the hook to take leave from work or pay for help out of pocket."
Robert
"Hold on — they pay her three thousand five hundred?"
Agent
"Three thousand five hundred, yes. Up to twice in your lifetime."
Robert
"That's… wow. That'd be a real help."
Agent
"Now here's where the math gets interesting. Plan B is about $53 a month. But the plan also reimburses your prescriptions — $10 every generic, $25 every brand. So your REAL out-of-pocket on the home healthcare side, after the prescription reimbursement, is about $3 a month."
Robert
"Wait — slow down. Three dollars a month?"
Agent
"Three to five, depending on the month. The premium is $53, but you're getting roughly $50 back in prescription reimbursement. Net cost is whatever's left. Does that make sense as how it works?"
The Rx-net-cost reframe — the moment of the close. The agent slows down because she heard Robert slow down. Real-time tonality matching. "Does that make sense?" is the validation check — and Robert's slow-down a moment earlier was the buying signal she was reading for.
Robert
"Yeah, that makes sense. So for hospital and home combined, what am I looking at?"
Agent
"Hospital is $35. Home Healthcare after the prescription benefit is $3 to $5. All-in: about $38 to $40 a month. For $1,500 to $9,000 in hospital cash if you get admitted, plus $100,000 in home healthcare if you ever need care, plus $3,500 directly to your daughter if she ends up helping."
Robert
"That's… yeah. That's a lot of coverage for not much money."
Stage 5 · Commitment 33:30
Agent
"So Robert — do you feel like this could be the answer for you?"
Robert
"Yeah, I do."
Agent
"Hold on — why do you feel like it is, though?"
The Q2 genius move. Most agents hear "yes" and rush to the application. NEPQ says: make him defend the yes. By the time Robert finishes explaining it back to her, he's sold himself harder than she ever could.
Robert
"Well, like you said — the hospital thing's a real worry. $1,500 in copays would hurt right now. And the home care, with the kids being so far away… if something happened to me, Linda couldn't do it all herself. Or vice versa. Having $3,500 to give my daughter so she can fly down without losing her income, that's huge. And the math just works — three or four bucks a month, I'd be crazy not to."
Agent
"What specific part of what we went over feels most important to you?"
Robert
"Honestly? The daughter piece. She's got two little kids, a job, and her own mortgage. I don't want her to be in a spot where helping us costs her her career."
Agent
"Yeah. Most folks who lock this in are doing it for exactly that reason. So the next step — I'd transfer you to one of our UNL specialists. They handle the application side, takes about 10 minutes. I'll stay on the line, introduce you, then step out. Once you're approved, you'll get your policy in the mail. Sound okay?"
Robert
"Yeah, let's do it."

▸ Post-call analysis

What the master agent did

Earned a $160 (HHC) + $112 (HI) = ~$272 take-home cross-sell on top of the MA close, in 20 minutes added to a 25-minute call. Two clean products. Zero SOA scope-creep. Customer left feeling protected, not sold.

The three moves to copy

  • "Last thing before I let you go" — leverages peak trust right when the customer thinks the call is wrapping. Frames the ancillary cross-sell as completion, not a separate pitch.
  • "Say more about that" on the Preference Question — extracted the emotional anchor ("I built this house in '92") that powered the entire close. Looping is the most under-practiced NEPQ move; it doubled the close strength here.
  • The Q2 commitment move — "Why do you feel it is, though?" — made Robert sell himself. By the time he finished explaining the daughter piece, the application was a formality.

The common mistake this avoided

Most agents who cross-sell after an MA close lead with "Now I want to tell you about Hospital Indemnity." That sounds like a separate pitch. The master move is "Last thing — your new plan still has gaps" — same products, completely different framing. The first sounds like upselling; the second sounds like completing the protection.

Customer's real monthly cost: ~$40 all-in. For $1,500-$9,000 in hospital indemnity cash + $100,000 in home healthcare coverage + $3,500 caregiver lump-sum to his daughter. About the price of one nice lunch at Publix.

Journey 1 · Path 1b · The expert move

Diane — the MA rescue

Diane called to switch MA plans. After 10 minutes of discovery, the agent confirms she's outside a valid enrollment period and can't switch. Most agents end the call here. The master agent doesn't. This script teaches the single highest-leverage move in your entire career: "Even though we can't change your plan today…"

Pre-call setup
Lead source
SMID-approved inbound (Medicare benefits creative)
Persona
Diane, 66, widow (husband Bill passed 18 months ago), FL panhandle
Household
Lives alone in the home she and Bill built; daughter Karen (40) lives in NJ, visits twice a year
Income
$1,400/mo Social Security · no pension · ~$8K savings
Prescriptions
4 generics monthly — HCTZ, atorvastatin, levothyroxine, sertraline
Current MA
Humana HMO · enrolled 7 months ago · no valid SEP available
Fit filters
No Medicaid · No Medigap · Both HI + HHC in play
Pre-call state
10 min in. Agent has just confirmed no SEP exists. Most agents would say goodbye here.
The dead-end 10:15
Agent
"…so unfortunately, Diane, since you enrolled in Humana 7 months ago and you're not in a valid Special Enrollment Period, I can't switch your plan today. You'd be looking at the Open Enrollment window in October."
Diane
"Oh… so I have to wait? I thought I could change it. The ad I saw made it sound like…"
Agent
"I hear you. The ads are confusing. For most folks the only times you can switch are during the fall open enrollment, or if you qualify for a Special Enrollment Period — moving, losing other coverage, things like that. None of those apply to your situation right now."
Diane
"Well, that's a bummer. I guess I'll just have to wait then…"
This is the moment. 90% of agents say "I'm sorry, please call back in October" and end the call. The master agent does not. She has 20 more minutes of value to deliver and Diane has no idea it's coming.
The pivot bridge 10:45
Agent
"Hang on, Diane — before you go. Even though we can't change your plan today, your current Humana plan still has a daily hospital copay if you ever get admitted, and Medicare itself doesn't cover daily-living help at home no matter which plan you're on. There's separate coverage that helps with both of those — works alongside your Humana plan, doesn't change anything about it. Couple of quick questions to see if it's something that fits?"
The pivot script — verbatim. Four moves in one sentence: (1) names the dead-end honestly, (2) reframes Diane's exposure as ongoing reality (not hypothetical), (3) introduces a NEW product that doesn't disturb her current coverage, (4) permission-asks with a time expectation. Total runtime of the pivot: 18 seconds. Result: she stays on the call.
Diane
"Oh… okay, yeah. What is it?"
Agent
"Let me ask you a few things first so I can make sure it's a fit. Tell me about your prescriptions — how many are you filling regularly?"
She doesn't name the products yet. NEPQ discipline: discover first, present last. Same playbook as Robert's call — but Diane doesn't realize she's in a discovery flow because she thinks the call was about to end.

▸ Post-call analysis

What the master agent did

Converted a dead MA call into a $272 cross-sell (HHC + HI). The call should have ended at 10:15 with "I'm sorry, please call back in October." Instead, it earned 25 minutes of additional value — for the agent AND for Diane, who is now protected against the exact two exposures she was most afraid of.

The three moves to copy

  • The pivot script verbatim — "Hang on, Diane — before you go. Even though we can't change your plan today, your current Humana plan still leaves you with…" This single sentence is worth more than any other in this Master Class. Memorize it. Use it on every MA call that hits a dead end.
  • The widow acknowledgment — Diane mentioned her husband off-hand in early discovery. The agent circled back at the right moment to honor it. This wasn't a sales move; it was just being human. It earned the trust that powered everything after.
  • The TCare-as-the-answer moment — the agent didn't sell harder when Diane said "that's exactly what I worry about." She let the moment breathe. Knowing when to stop talking is one of the highest skills in selling.

The common mistake this avoided

The mistake isn't selling Diane the wrong product. The mistake is ENDING THE CALL at the dead-end. Most agents treat "no SEP available" as a hard stop. They've trained themselves to think of MA as the only thing on the table. The master agent treats the SEP rejection as an invitation to pivot — because Diane's exposure (hospital costs + home-care gap + isolated widow with distant daughter) was never about her plan. It was about her life. The plan change wouldn't have helped any of it. The HI + HHC cross-sell solves all of it.

Customer's real monthly cost: ~$45 all-in. For $1,500-$2,500 in hospital indemnity cash + $100,000 in home healthcare coverage + $3,500 directly to Karen if she ever helps. Diane left this call feeling lighter — not because she bought something, but because she put a real safety net in place for the daughter she was most worried about.

What Diane will tell Karen tonight: "I talked to a really nice woman today. We can't change my Medicare plan until October, but she helped me put two policies in place — if I end up in the hospital or needing help at home, I'm covered. And there's even something that pays you if you ever have to come down." That call will be the moment Karen tells her friends about CPC — and the reason the next referral comes in.

▸ Post-call analysis

What the master agent did

Declined the HI sale James asked for, and earned a Plan C HHC sale (~$235 take-home) instead. The customer specifically thanked her at the end — "Says a lot" — and that one comment is the seed of every future referral James generates. Lazy agents would have sold HI on top of Plan G and generated a 90-day refund call.

The three moves to copy

  • The fit-filter question in the first 30 seconds — "What's your current Medicare situation?" — caught Medigap before any pitching happened. This single question is the lowest-effort, highest-leverage discovery move in the playbook.
  • The honest decline — "Hospital Indemnity wouldn't really add anything for you." Walking away from a sale to keep trust intact is the most counterintuitive move in selling. It feels like leaving money on the table; it actually generates the next two sales (Plan C now, future referrals later).
  • The Plan C math walkthrough — explaining WHY Plan C fits his persona (heavy brand-prescription load = bigger reimbursement potential = bigger tier makes sense) treated James as a peer, not a mark. He bought because he understood the math, not because he was sold.

The common mistake this avoided

The mistake isn't pitching HI to a Medigap holder — that's a known compliance no-no anyway. The deeper mistake is selling Plan B when Plan C fits. Agents who default everyone to Plan B miss the prescription-cap math: heavy brand-filler customers like James leave $25 a month on the table at Plan B that Plan C captures. Plan C's higher premium is mostly offset by the higher reimbursement cap, and the coverage difference ($150K vs $100K) is real protection. Knowing when to upgrade-tier is part of the master craft.

Customer's real monthly cost: ~$3 on Plan C. For $150,000 in home healthcare coverage + $450/day daily benefit + $3,500 caregiver lump-sum + the option to repeat once more in his lifetime. James will tell his neighbor at the golf club tomorrow.

An emotionally devastated caregiver came in looking for something to help his wife and left with something to help his whole family — including himself, which he wasn't even considering. The TCare-to-David framing was the closer.

The three moves to copy

  • Empathy before anything — the first 30 seconds were just acknowledgment. No qualifying questions, no fit-filter, no products mentioned. Frank needed to feel heard before he could even hear what the agent was offering.
  • The honest reframe — telling Frank the policy doesn't cover Margaret directly was the moment most agents would soften or evade. The agent stated it plainly. That honesty earned the trust that made the protection-the-protector pivot work.
  • Plan C recommendation with reasoning — the agent didn't default to Plan B because it was the "common fit." She explicitly explained why Plan C made sense for a caregiver of an Alzheimer's patient: higher caregiver burnout risk = higher likelihood of needing the bigger coverage. Frank bought Plan C because the agent treated him like an engineer who could handle the analysis.

The common mistake this avoided

The mistake is letting an emotional caregiver lead you into an emotional sale that gets unraveled later. Frank could have been talked into Plan B at $8/mo "to save money" — and three months later, when he's drowning in caregiving and realizes he might actually need care himself, he'd kick himself for not going Plan C. The master move is to spend the extra 90 seconds explaining tier selection so the customer chooses with their head while their heart is in the right place. Plan C ends up being the right choice because the reasoning makes sense, not because the agent pushed.

What the master agent did

Closed Plan B HHC (~$160 take-home) in 11 minutes 30 seconds total — including the 7-minute application. The actual sales call was 4 minutes 30 seconds. That's possible because: the form did the qualifying, the speed-to-lead preserved the customer's attention, and the agent compressed the NEPQ stages without skipping any of them. High-intent leads convert at materially higher rates than inbound general-benefit, but only if you respect the compression.

The three moves to copy

  • The 45-seconds-ago opener — naming the timing honestly is what makes the speed feel like service, not stalking. "Wanted to follow up while you're still thinking about it" is the perfect framing for a sub-minute callback.
  • Knockout verification in one sentence — Eleanor passed the knockouts on the form. Re-asking them as separate questions would have signaled distrust of her form responses and slowed the call. One verification line covers all of them.
  • Math first, not last — on a high-intent lead, the math is the lead. Eleanor came in for the Rx reimbursement piece — the agent leaned into that immediately and built around it. Standard NEPQ scripts that delay math to Stage 4 work for general-benefit leads but are slow on a 60-second callback.

The common mistake this avoided

The mistake on a 60-second callback is treating it like a normal inbound call. Standard NEPQ pacing — 8-10 minutes of discovery before math — kills the energy of a high-intent lead. By minute 6, Eleanor would have lost interest. The master move is recognizing that pre-qualification has already happened, and the call's job is to confirm + close, not to re-run the full method. Speed honors the intent. Slowness on a sub-minute callback feels like the agent doesn't trust the form data.
