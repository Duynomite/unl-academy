1 Pillar 1

Read the Customer

The audio intelligence beneath the script. What separates agents who run conversations from agents who follow scripts.

A great script delivered by an agent who can't read the room is just noise. This pillar trains the underlying perception — tonality, pace, pause, the specific phrases that signal a buyer is leaning in or pulling back. By the end, you'll know how to adjust your delivery in real time based on what you're hearing.

1.1 · The seven-second test
4 min
What you can tell about a prospect in their first seven seconds — and how to adjust your opener accordingly.

By the time the prospect says hello and one short sentence, you already have four pieces of data. Most agents miss them because they're rehearsing their script. The agents who listen first close more.

What you can read in seven seconds

  • Voice quality. Clear vs. muffled, energetic vs. flat, formal vs. casual. Tells you their state.
  • First phrase. "Yeah?" vs. "This is Robert" vs. "Hello, who's calling?" Tells you their guard level.
  • Breath pattern. Steady vs. hurried vs. labored. Tells you whether they have time and headspace.
  • Background context. TV in the background, traffic, a dog barking, silence. Tells you what else is competing for their attention.

How to adjust your opener

  • Hurried or agitated → "Did I catch you at a bad time?" Always — even if you think you didn't.
  • Flat or passive → Match their low energy. Don't try to lift them with your enthusiasm; they'll perceive it as pressure.
  • Formal (uses "Mr." or "Mrs." themselves) → Mirror the formality through the whole call. Don't get familiar.
  • Suspicious or edgy → Lead with compliance signals: "This is a recorded line — I'm a licensed agent calling back the request you submitted."
The doctrine The first seven seconds aren't selling time — they're listening time. Every agent who rushes through hello to get to their script is paying a tax they don't know they're paying.
1.2 · Tonality matching
5 min
Mirror pace, energy, formality. The senior-call principle: always pace below the prospect, never above.

At ages 65 to 85, average comprehension speed is roughly 30% slower than at 45. If you talk at your normal sales pace, the prospect is processing the first half of your sentence while you're already three sentences ahead. They're not slow — they're operating at normal age-cognition speed against unnatural agent pace.

Senior-call principle: always pace below the prospect, never above. Match their tempo at the start, then gently pull the whole conversation down by 10-15%. They'll follow you without noticing, and they'll feel heard the entire way.

Same line, two ways

Eager-fast (avoid)
No pauses, permission tucked away as a formality

Agent — quick, upbeatHi Mr. Hendricks this is Sarah I'm calling about the Medicare benefits I noticed you signed up for so I just wanted to double check a few things real quick is that okay?

Calm-expert (use)
Clear pauses, real permission gate, respectful out

Agent — soft, pacedHi Mr. Hendricks. [pause 1s] This is Sarah from Clear Path Coverage. [pause 0.5s] Quick courtesy call — [match his pace] do you have a minute, or did I catch you at a bad time?

The difference comes down to breath. The eager-fast version forces you to rush — by the end you're almost gasping. The calm-expert version lets you breathe between phrases. Your prospect can hear that difference even if they can't name it.

Three pacing techniques

  • Match-and-pace. Start at their tempo. Over the first 60 seconds, pull the conversation down 10-15%. They follow without noticing.
  • The deliberate pause. A one-second pause after a question forces the prospect to fill it. Agents who fear silence kill their own discovery.
  • Soften the stress. When you're about to say something the prospect might resist — price, knockout question, anything sensitive — drop your volume slightly. Senior prospects hear "louder" as "pushier."
The most common mistake Matching their nervous energy upward instead of pulling it down. If a prospect sounds anxious, don't match anxious — anchor calm. You're not mirroring their state; you're modeling the state you want them to settle into.
1.3 · Buying signals — the eight verbal cues
5 min
Specific phrases and behaviors that mean "they're ready." How to recognize them and pivot from pitch to handoff.

Most agents miss buying signals because they're still selling. The signal lands, the agent keeps pitching benefits, the prospect's open window closes, and the agent never knows why the call dried up. When you hear any of the eight cues below, your job switches from "convince" to "execute the handoff."

1
"How does this work exactly?"
MoveStop talking about benefits. Start describing the application steps and what happens next.
2
"What would my monthly be?"
MoveDeliver the Rx-net-cost reframe. Don't lead with gross premium — pre-frame it as the number that doesn't matter, then land the net.
3
"Could I add my wife too?"
MoveConfirm she's also on Medicare. Then pivot to a brief spouse discovery — her Rx mix, her health, separate underwriting.
4
"When would it start?"
MoveExplain the effective date plainly. Pre-set the underwriting and Advocate handoff so the transition feels expected, not jarring.
5
"What happens if I cancel?"
MoveAddress it freely — no long-term commitment, free-look period, money back. Then turn to the next step. Hesitating signals there's something to hide.
6
"Hmm — that's not bad."
MoveMicro-close: "Do you feel this is the answer for you?" Don't keep selling. They've already nodded internally.
7
"What do you need from me?"
MoveAssumptive transition to the Advocate handoff. They're already past the decision — your job is to make the next step painless.
8
A pause of three or more seconds after you describe the benefit.
MoveDon't fill it. They're internalizing — let them speak first. The agent who waits four seconds wins the sale more often than the agent who jumps in at two.
The pattern beneath the signals The fastest way to lose a buying signal is to keep selling past it. When you hear one, your job changes — you're no longer convincing, you're executing. Recognize the shift and let the close happen.
1.4 · Resistance signals — when they're pulling back
5 min
The six cues of disengagement. Defensive contractions, time-stalling, vague answers — and the recovery move for each.

Resistance is data, not failure. The six cues below tell you exactly where rapport broke — and each has a specific recovery move. Recovery means rebuilding rapport before re-pitching, never re-pitching over the resistance.

1
Defensive contractions. They shift from "I'm" to "I am," casual to formal. They're putting up a wall.
RecoverySoften your tone immediately. Ask an open question that hands them control: "What's on your mind?"
2
Time-stalling. "I'll have to think about it," "let me get back to you," "I'd want to talk to my wife first."
RecoverySurface the real hesitation: "I get that — is there a specific piece that's still unclear, or is it more that you want to mull it over?"
3
Vague answers to specific questions. "How many medications?" → "A few." "How often?" → "Sometimes."
RecoveryGentle precision: "By a few — do you mean two or three, or more like five or six?" Pin the number without sounding like an interrogation.
4
Repeating questions you already answered. They weren't listening the first time.
RecoveryDon't repeat the same answer. Reframe it through their own words: "You mentioned the copay was your biggest worry — here's specifically how this addresses that."
5
"What's the catch?" or "What's the cost?" up front. They've been burned before. The defensiveness is about prior agents, not you.
RecoveryAddress the suspicion directly: "Honest answer — yes, there's a cost. Let me show you the math, then you decide if it's worth it."
6
Talking over you mid-sentence. They're cognitively saturated — you've put too much in front of them too fast.
RecoveryStop. Let them finish. Acknowledge what they said. Then ask: "What's the part that's still on your mind?" Slow the call back down.
The temptation to push through When resistance hits, most agents push harder — more benefits, faster delivery, louder enthusiasm. That's the move that closes the window for good. Resistance is a request to slow down and re-establish rapport. Honor it and you keep the call. Push through it and you lose it.
1.5 · The "calm expert" persona
4 min
Internalizing the NEPQ-required tone. Not what calm sounds like — what calm thinks like.

Agents who try to perform calm sound performative. Agents who actually feel calm sound calm. The way to feel calm on a call you've made a hundred times is to know what's happening before the prospect speaks — and to hold the right internal posture before you open your mouth.

This unit teaches the internal state that produces the right delivery. Get the state right and the tone follows naturally.

Before you speak, your internal posture is: I'm curious, not selling. I'm present, not performing. The prospect isn't an obstacle — they're someone who needs information I have. My job is to find out if this fits. If it doesn't, I'll DQ kindly. If it does, the math sells itself. Either way, I'm here to listen first.

When you hold that posture before you pick up the phone, the opener writes itself. The pace settles automatically. The prospect feels the difference in the first ten seconds — and you didn't have to perform a thing.

Three components of the calm-expert state

  • Curiosity over conviction. Replace "I need to close this" with "I want to know if this fits." Curiosity-led tone is naturally calmer than conviction-led tone — and conviction-led tone is what prospects experience as pressure.
  • Presence over performance. Most agents are mentally one step ahead of the conversation — already scripting the next move while the prospect is still talking. Presence means listening with full attention, even on the hundredth call where you already know the answer.
  • Service over outcome. The prospect can tell whether you care about their fit or your sale. The way to actually care: assume each prospect might be a graceful DQ. Then when they're not, you've earned the close honestly.
Where it comes from The "calm expert" isn't fake calm. It's the calm that comes from knowing the framework so well you don't have to perform expertise. The Crash Course gave you the framework. Now run calls until it feels automatic. That's when calm-expert becomes who you are, not what you're trying to sound like.
1.6 · Reading silence
3 min
When to wait. When to fill. When to ask a clarifying question. The discipline most agents never develop.

Silence on a senior call is almost never empty. It's processing. Most agents fear it and fill it — that's how you talk yourself out of sales you already had.

Three silence types

  • Processing silence (2-5 seconds after you describe something). They're absorbing. Wait. Filling it interrupts the very thinking you need them to do.
  • Resistance silence (5+ seconds, often after a price reveal). They're deciding whether to push back. Soften your tone, then ask gently: "What's coming up for you?"
  • Disengagement silence (10+ seconds, no breath cues, energy gone). They've checked out. Re-engage gently — never with more selling: "Mr. Hendricks, are you still with me?"
The default rule Count to four before you fill any pause. If they speak before four, they were processing — let them. If they don't speak by four, ask a clarifying question. Never re-pitch into silence. The agent who can comfortably sit in a three-second pause has more leverage than the agent who can't. Silence makes prospects work — and the prospect doing the work is the prospect leaning in.
1.7 · The mid-call self-check
3 min
A 4-question internal audit you run on yourself at minute 5 of every call. Stops you from missing what's actually happening.

At roughly the five-minute mark of every call, run a quick internal audit. Most agents drift without realizing it — these four questions catch the drift in about ten seconds and let you correct mid-call instead of after.

  1. Am I still in discovery, or did I slip into pitching too early? If they haven't told me their Rx mix yet, I'm not ready to present. Go back.
  2. Is my pace still below theirs? If I've sped up, I'm getting eager. Pull it back down a notch.
  3. Have I asked an open question in the last 90 seconds? If not, I've slipped into monologue. Ask one now.
  4. What's their energy compared to three minutes ago? Up = leaning in, keep going. Down = losing them, slow down and re-engage. Flat = ambivalent — pivot to a deeper question.
The discipline The best agents don't run perfect calls. They run calls with continuous mid-course corrections. The five-minute self-check is the simplest version of that discipline — and the agents who build it into muscle memory outperform agents who only review their calls after the fact.

▸ Post-call analysis

What the master agent did

Earned a $160 (HHC) + $112 (HI) = ~$272 take-home cross-sell on top of the MA close, in 20 minutes added to a 25-minute call. Two clean products. Zero SOA scope-creep. Customer left feeling protected, not sold.

The three moves to copy

  • "Last thing before I let you go" — leverages peak trust right when the customer thinks the call is wrapping. Frames the ancillary cross-sell as completion, not a separate pitch.
  • "Say more about that" on the Preference Question — extracted the emotional anchor ("I built this house in '92") that powered the entire close. Looping is the most under-practiced NEPQ move; it doubled the close strength here.
  • The Q2 commitment move — "Why do you feel it is, though?" — made Robert sell himself. By the time he finished explaining the daughter piece, the application was a formality.

The common mistake this avoided

Most agents who cross-sell after an MA close lead with "Now I want to tell you about Hospital Indemnity." That sounds like a separate pitch. The master move is "Last thing — your new plan still has gaps" — same products, completely different framing. The first sounds like upselling; the second sounds like completing the protection.

3.4 · The "I want to talk to my spouse" stall
4 min
When it's real, when it's a stall, and the verbatim move for each.

"Let me talk to my wife/husband" is two completely different things wearing the same words. One is a real consultation pattern. The other is a polite no. Reading which is which is half the unit.

How to tell them apart

  • Real consultation. Prospect has been actively engaged. Asked thoughtful questions. Took notes. Mentioned the spouse organically earlier ("my wife usually handles the bills"). Sounds energized when they bring it up.
  • Stall. Prospect has been disengaged from minute one. Hasn't asked a question. "Spouse" appears suddenly as the call gets specific. Sounds relieved when they bring it up.
When it's real
"My wife and I make these decisions together — I'd want to talk to her first."
"That makes sense. Most couples handle this kind of decision together. What works for me — I'll send a one-page summary of what we covered, and I'll call back to walk through any questions she has. Does Tuesday or Wednesday work better?"
Why this worksHonors the process. Pre-books the next step instead of leaving it floating. The take-home summary makes the prospect look organized to their spouse — a small social win.
When it's a stall
[Disengaged the whole call, hasn't asked a question — "spouse" appears as the exit door.]
"I hear you. Honest question — is this something you and your spouse actually want to talk about, or is calling her back a way of telling me politely that it's not for you?"
Why this worksPermission to say no. Most stallers say "yeah, honestly it's not for me, sorry" and you save 40 minutes of follow-up chasing nothing. The minority who say "no, I really do want to talk to her" become real callbacks.
3.5 · The skeptical-of-insurance prospect
4 min
A trust-building macro pattern. Long arc, careful delivery, payoff at the close.

Some prospects don't object to this product — they object to insurance as a category. They've been oversold before. They watched a relative get burned. They view every agent as a salesperson first and a human second. You can't win this objection in a single move. You win it across the whole call.

Three components, in order

  • Don't argue. Skepticism gets stronger when you push against it. Acknowledge that they have reason to be skeptical. "Most insurance products are oversold. Fair to be cautious."
  • Earn micro-trust early — give them one piece of straight information that costs you something. Example: "Most ancillary policies are oversold to people who don't really need them. Let me show you when this one makes sense and when it doesn't." The willingness to talk them out of it builds more trust than any benefit list could.
  • Let them ask. Skeptics respond to inquiry, not pitch. Once the trust starts forming, stop describing benefits and ask them: "What would make this worth even considering?" Their answer tells you exactly what to say next.
The non-obvious finding Skeptics close at higher rates than enthusiasts once you earn their trust. Enthusiasts are easy to start with and easy to lose at the end. Skeptics are hard to start with and hard to lose at the end. The mistake is trying to overcome skepticism with enthusiasm. Match calm with calm. Inquiry with inquiry.

"Is this a scam? How did you get my number?"
"Totally fair question. Honest answer — no, this isn't a scam. I'm a licensed agent, this is a recorded line, and the company I represent is [carrier]. I'm calling about the request that came in [where the lead came from]. Want to take 60 seconds to verify me, or want me to keep going so we can see if this is even relevant for you?"
Why this worksAddresses the question directly. Offers a verification path. Ends with a permission gate. Three trust signals in one breath.

What kills it

  • Sounding offended. The prospect isn't insulting you personally — they're protecting themselves. React like it's a reasonable question.
  • Over-explaining. Skeptics smell defensiveness. Short, factual, calm.
  • Skipping the permission gate. The offer to stop the call is the move that earns the call. Without it, you're still in their "pushy" category.
3.7 · Time pressure — "I only have five minutes"
3 min
Two paths: respect the limit, or earn more time. The signal that tells you which.

"I only have five minutes" is sometimes literal and sometimes a test. The difference is the prospect's tone and context. Read it before you choose your move.

Path A · Respect it
[They sound genuinely rushed. Background noise. Kids. Work in the background.]
"Got it. Let me make these five minutes count. The fastest path is for me to ask you two questions, give you a yes/no on fit, and either book a longer call or wrap it up. Sound okay?"
Why this worksCompressed discovery, honest framing, clear exit. You earn the next call by respecting this one. Run the most compressed version of stage 2 you can — two questions, fit verdict, next step.
Path B · Earn more
[They sound calm and focused. The "five minutes" sounds like a boundary test, not a real constraint.]
"Five is tight for what we'd cover. Honest question — is the five-minute limit firm, or if I made it valuable in the first three would you give me more?"
Why this worksCalls the test without being rude. Most prospects who set artificial limits will extend if you respect them enough to ask. The ones who don't were never going to convert anyway.
3.8 · Emotional curveballs — crying, venting, recent loss
4 min
When the prospect's emotional state takes over the call. How to be present without losing the call.

Senior calls touch grief, fear, and loss more than any other sales category. A prospect might mention a sick spouse, a recent death, or a financial fear that breaks them open mid-call. The agent who treats these moments as obstacles loses the call. The agent who treats them as invitations to be human earns it.

Three types, three moves

Type 1 · Crying or breaking down
[Often triggered by a sick spouse, recent loss, or sudden awareness of financial exposure.]
"Take a moment. — We can absolutely come back to this another day if today isn't right."
Why this worksPause the call. Don't move forward until they invite it. The offer to reschedule signals you're not just here to close. Some will accept; some will say "no, please keep going."
Type 2 · Venting
[About Medicare, the system, prior agents, life in general.]
[Listen. Don't redirect. After they finish:] "I hear you. That sounds exhausting. Want me to keep going with this, or is today not the day?"
Why this worksVenting is rapport currency the prospect is giving you. Cutting it short costs more than the time it saves. The check-in question after they finish honors what they shared.
Type 3 · Recent loss
[Spouse, child, parent — often shared in stage 2 discovery as context.]
"I'm so sorry. — Some people in your shoes find this helpful because it's one less thing to worry about. Others find it's too much on top of everything else. Which side are you on right now?"
Why this worksDirect acknowledgment, then two seconds of silence (the pause matters). The "which side are you on" question lets them choose without pressure — and they'll tell you honestly.
The deeper rule Sales-mode and grief-mode are mutually exclusive. Switch fully or not at all. The agent who half-acknowledges the moment and then pivots back to pitch is worse than the agent who never acknowledged it. If you're going to be human, be all the way human.
3.9 · The "send me information" brushoff
3 min
Why "send me info" almost always means no. The two moves that convert it into a continued conversation.

"Just send me some information and I'll look it over" sounds polite. It almost always means "I don't want to say no on the phone." Email-only follow-ups land at near-zero conversion. Letting the prospect go quiet is letting the sale die.

Move A · Surface the brushoff (when rapport feels low)
"Just send me some information and I'll look it over."
"I can send something, but most people who get the email never open it — they just wanted off the call politely. No judgment if that's where you are. But if there's actual interest, the email isn't going to make a decision easier. What's the part that's not landing for you?"
Why this worksCalls the brushoff gently. Gives them permission to say no. If they're really not interested, the call ends honestly. If they are, the real objection comes out.
Move B · Convert to a live read (when rapport feels okay)
"Could you send me something I can look over?"
"Here's what I'll do — I'll send a one-page summary right now while we're on the line. While you have it open, I can answer any specific question that comes up as you read it. Faster than reading it alone. Want to do it that way?"
Why this worksHonors the request but keeps the conversation alive. The prospect gets to read AND ask. Most accept because it's actually more efficient for them.
The graveyard Never let "send me info" be the close. The email goes unopened, the follow-up call goes to voicemail, the lead dies. Either surface what's really happening with Move A or convert to a live read with Move B. Email-only is where good leads go to die.
4 Pillar 4

Close with Confidence

The art of recognizing "ready to close" and what happens after. Most training stops at "they said yes." Mastery includes setting up the post-sale relationship that produces persistency.

A sale that cancels in month 3 hurts the agent (chargeback) and CPC (CTM risk). This pillar teaches the close itself — the micro-moves and the macro-timing — and what to say in the 60 seconds after approval that determines whether the customer becomes a long-term policyholder or a 90-day cancellation.

4.1 · Recognizing "ready to close"
4 min
The macro state shift that tells you the prospect crossed from interested to ready. How to stop selling the moment you see it.

Pillar 1 taught you the eight individual buying signals — phrases that mean "ready" in a single moment. This unit is about the bigger pattern: the energy shift that tells you the prospect has crossed a line, even before they say a specific buying signal.

Three macro indicators

  • The energy shift. Their questions go from "what does it cover" to "what's next." They've stopped evaluating. They're starting to plan.
  • The "we" pronoun appearing. "If we had this..." or "we'd just need to..." They're already imagining themselves with the policy. The decision happened internally before they said anything.
  • The shift from theoretical to practical. "When would coverage start?" "What does my wife need to do?" "Do I need to do this on a computer?" The questions get logistical.
The mistake Continuing to sell past the shift. Agents who don't recognize the energy change keep listing benefits, and the prospect's tone flattens — they were ready, and you kept presenting past ready. Flat tone after engaged tone means you missed the close. Course-correct by stopping mid-sentence and asking: "Where's your head at right now?"
4.2 · The micro-close — when to use, when it's premature
3 min
"Do you feel this is the answer for you?" lands when timing is right. Breaks rapport when premature. The four checks before you ask it.

The micro-close is a low-pressure check-in. Asked at the right moment, it advances the call. Asked too early, it makes you sound transactional. Run the four checks below first. If any is missing, go shore up the missing piece before you ask.

The four pre-checks

  1. They've heard the math. Gross premium and net cost both. If they've only heard one number, they're not ready.
  2. Knockouts are clean. You've worked through the health questions and nothing fired. If anything is unresolved, the micro-close is premature.
  3. They've asked at least one specific question. Their question signals engagement. Without it, you don't have the data to know if you're close.
  4. Their tone is leaning in. Energy up, not flat. Curious, not polite-dismissive.
When all four are green Just ask. Direct, calm, no preamble: "Do you feel this is the answer for you?" Then stop talking. The agent who fears the silence after that question kills the close more often than the agent who asks too early.
4.3 · The double commit — Q1 / Q2 in full
4 min
The Crash Course sketched this. Here's the full delivery — and the non-obvious Q2 move that makes prospects sell themselves harder than you could.

Once the micro-close lands with a yes, most agents go straight to application. The masters add one more move first: the second commit question. It feels small. It moves persistency more than any other single move in the call.

Q1 · The commit
[Run the four pre-checks. Then:]
"Do you feel this is the answer for you?"
Why this worksIt's the lightest possible commitment ask. Yes/no, no pressure, no implication that they're locked in.
Q2 · The non-obvious move
[They say yes. Don't move to application yet. Instead:]
"Tell me — why do you feel it is?"
Why this worksThey sell themselves. Their answer becomes the reason they remember in month 4 when they're tempted to cancel. The agent's reasons fade; the prospect's own reasons stick.
What kills Q2 Adding your own reasons after they answer. They said: "Because if my wife ever needs help at home, I don't want to scramble." Your move is "That's exactly right" — not "Right, and also it covers X, Y, Z." Let their reason be the reason. Anything you add dilutes the commitment they just made.
4.4 · The application handoff
3 min
The Advocate transfer is a high-risk moment. Done well, it preserves the sale. Done poorly, it loses it.

You're handing the warm prospect to a stranger. The wrong handoff feels like being dropped. The right one feels like a continuation. Three rules govern what you do.

The transfer line
[After Q2, before you bring the Advocate on:]
"I'm going to bring on our licensed Advocate, [name]. They'll walk through the application with you and answer any compliance questions. I'll stay on the line in case anything comes up. This usually takes 8 to 12 minutes."
Why this worksNames the Advocate. Sets time expectation. Promises you stay. The prospect doesn't feel transferred to a stranger and abandoned — they feel handed off carefully.

Three rules while the Advocate talks

  • Stay quiet most of the time. The Advocate has a script. Talking over them creates compliance issues and slows the call down.
  • Chime in only to clarify, never to add. If the prospect asks something the Advocate phrases technically, you can translate. Don't volunteer new information.
  • Be audibly present at start and end. Say one short thing at the beginning ("I'm right here with you"). Say one short thing at approval ("Congratulations — this is set"). In between, you're a quiet presence, not a participant.
4.5 · The post-sale 60 seconds
3 min
Most agents end the call when the application is signed. The 60 seconds after approval is where you protect the sale.

Approval lands. Most agents say "great, we're all set" and end the call. The agents with the strongest persistency use the next 60 seconds to set expectations that prevent surprise — and surprise is the biggest driver of early cancellation.

Four things to cover, in order

  1. Policy delivery timing. "You'll get the policy in the mail or by email in [timeframe]. Look for [carrier name]."
  2. First premium expectations. "Your first payment will come out on [date]. After that, it's [monthly/annual] on the same date."
  3. Claims contact info. "If you ever need to file a claim, the number is on the policy. You can also call me back and I'll walk you through it."
  4. The welcome call from the carrier. "Within the next [X] days, you'll get a quick call from [carrier] just confirming you're satisfied. It's a normal verification call. Just say yes — they're checking to make sure I represented things correctly."
Why the welcome-call mention matters most A surprised welcome call where the prospect doesn't remember who you were or what they bought is the leading indicator of a 30-day cancellation. Mention the welcome call so it feels expected, not random. Two sentences here save dozens of chargebacks per year.
4.6 · Setting up persistency from minute one
4 min
Persistency isn't a post-sale event. The agent who sets it up during the call has far fewer chargebacks than the one who closes hard and hopes.

A policy that cancels in month 3 hurts everyone. Chargebacks for the agent. CTM exposure for CPC. The work of preventing this happens during the call, not after. Three small moves baked into normal call language move persistency the most.

Three persistency-building moves during the call

  • Frame the policy as "active starting [date]," not just "your new policy." The specific date creates a mental anchor. The prospect remembers when coverage began — and that's the day they'll measure against if they ever question the value.
  • Acknowledge the free-look period without highlighting it. "You'll have [X] days to review the policy when it arrives." Said once, in passing. Don't sell against it; just establish it as normal.
  • Mention the claims process casually so it feels accessible. "When you ever need to use this, you'll call [number]." Not "if you ever need to" — when. Subtle, but it pre-frames using the policy as inevitable, not hypothetical.
The deeper principle People cancel policies they don't feel ownership of. Each of these three moves transfers ownership to the prospect — the date is theirs, the review period is theirs, the future claim is theirs. You're not selling them a policy; you're handing them something they already own.
4.7 · The referral ask
3 min
Most agents never ask. The ones who do, ask wrong. The right moment and the right phrasing.

Referrals from satisfied prospects are the cheapest leads you'll ever get. Timing and phrasing decide whether you get one or kill the goodwill of the sale. Both are small. Both matter more than agents realize.

The right moment

After the Q2 commit, before the Advocate handoff. They're in "yes" mode. The decision is fresh. They feel good about it. Not during the application (interrupts compliance flow). Not after the welcome call (too late, momentum gone). Not as a follow-up text the next day (cold).

The ask
[Right after Q2. Tone is light, brief, casual.]
"While I have you — do you have a friend or family member who might want to know about this? No pressure, just a name."
Why this works"No pressure, just a name" lowers the ask. Most agents over-ask, framing it as a favor. This frames it as a small piece of information you might find useful. Easier to give.

If they give a name

  • Don't ask for the number on the spot. Too transactional. Instead: "Got it. I'll reach out and just mention you thought of them — that okay?"
  • Confirm what to say. "When I call, is it okay to mention your name, or would you prefer I not say where the referral came from?"
  • Thank, briefly. One sentence. Not a long thank-you that makes them feel they did something burdensome.
If they say no "No problem at all" — and move straight to the Advocate handoff. Don't push, don't soften, don't reframe. The ask cost you nothing; the gracious accept of "no" costs you nothing either. The prospect who said no today might send one in three months once they've used the policy.

Reference 3 · Layer 0 filters
CPC fit filters

Before underwriting, two CPC-specific filters run in the first 60 seconds of discovery. Medicaid is a do-not-sell rule (chargeback exposure). Medigap means HI doesn't add value — HHC only.

Medicaid · do-not-sell

Discovery question
"Are you on Medicaid at all, or just Medicare?"
Why this existsMedicaid customers cancel at much higher rates — financial strain leads to lapse, which means chargebacks on the agent and CTM exposure for CPC. This is a business rule, not an underwriting one. Graceful DQ at ~3 minutes is the right move.

Medigap · skip HI, pitch HHC only

Discovery question
"Do you have a Medicare Supplement plan — Medigap — or Medicare Advantage, or just Original Medicare?"
Why this mattersMedigap already covers the hospital copay HI Shield targets — it adds no value. But Medigap can't fill the home-care gap (HHC's territory). So Medigap holders are HHC-only customers, not cross-sells. Pitching HI to a Medigap customer makes you look like you don't understand their coverage.

Reference 6 · The guardrails
Compliance quick-reference

CPC's inbound-only model is structurally compliant by design. These are the phrases you avoid and the language you use instead. For verbatim TPMO disclaimer + SOA scripts, see the compliance playbook in the agent portal.

The three never-says

Never say: "long-term care insurance" / "LTC plan"
Say: "This is a supplemental indemnity policy that pays cash benefits during a period of home care."
Never say: "You'll save money on prescriptions" / "prescription savings program"
Say: "The plan reimburses $10 per generic and $25 per brand-name fill, up to your tier's annual cap. For a member with regular prescriptions, that offsets most of the premium."
Never say: "This replaces Medicare" / "better than Medicare"
Say: "This is a supplemental policy. Benefits are paid as outlined in the policy. This does not change or replace your Medicare coverage."

Verbal SOA

Capture a verbal SOA at the top of every call that enumerates every product category you might discuss — MA, MAPD, PDP, HI, HHC. The customer says yes/no to each. The Advocate's compliance script covers this in detail; you don't need to memorize the exact wording, but you do need to know why it happens: it protects the customer's right to choose what gets pitched, and it protects you from MCMG violations later.

Recording disclosure

Every call opens with the recording disclosure: "This call is being recorded for quality and compliance purposes." Said once, at the top, calmly. Don't bury it in a longer sentence — the prospect should clearly hear it.

The deeper rule Compliance isn't an obstacle — it's a shield. The agents who treat it as protection (theirs and the customer's) close at higher rates than the agents who treat it as red tape. CPC's inbound-only model is structurally one of the most compliant TPMO postures CMS allows. Lean into that.
